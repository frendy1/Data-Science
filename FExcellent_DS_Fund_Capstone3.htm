A golf fan's virtual view of the action on the green

Frendy Excellent, Thinkful, 4/1/2019


For a specific hole of a particular golf course, does the golf ball have a tendency to settle at a certain location or locations on the green, as a result of a putt? To answer this question, the investigation targeted two types of putts: putts driven into the 16th hole and putts missed (e.g., putts that overshot into the hole). A PGA ShotLink dataset for the 16th hole at the Waste Management Phoenix Open was analyzed. The ShotLink dataset includes GPS-like coordinates to help pinpoint the golf ball locations on the course. The 16th hole dataset investigation targeted all 16th hole green putts (for all rounds played) in year 2015, 2016, and 2017 Waste Management Phoenix Open. Note that even the elevation at sea level is present in the ShotLink dataset. This could be an important piece of data for future putt investigations, judging from the intimidating geography of the 16th hole (and high-decibel crowd). Mounds and slopes have been ignored here for the sake of simplicity. However, they probably play a significant role in the match. Nonetheless, since a picture is worth more than words, we will look a few charts to digest potentials sources of putting effects below.

 

 

 

 Holed Out Putts

The 2015, 2016, and 2017 Waste Management Phoenix Open competition resulted in 1,211 putts making it into the 16th hole (holed out putts). Furthermore, those putts consist of 595 far holed out putts and 616 near holed out putts, for a total 1211 such putts. The putts that made it into the hole exhibit two clusters. What have we found so far?

The above investigation indicates that holed out putts display patterns as to green location, while missed putts show no apparent location preference. So, the data has revealed, thus far, eight locations on the green were successful putts have a tendency to settle, while two additional locations on the green result in missed putts. What is needed is a bird’s eye view of the action on the green.  To gain valuable insights (using a “view from above”), a polar viewpoint of the data was adopted, as shown below.

 

 

 

 

It appears that some golfers who placed the ball into the 16th hole had a tendency to land their putts on the right side of the 16th hole and farther as well. Several unsuccessful distant putts can be seen on the right side of the hole. Thus, it is probably fair to propose that the right-sided putts are activities resulting from struggling golfers. Putts not landing on the right side of the hole are perhaps made by seasoned golfers, whom are probably not deviating much from the fairway. Furthermore, common sense dictates that closer putts are more likely to be successful, as well distant putts launched by seasoned golfers.

 

As you are aware, we must inspect the following three charts to figure out the real story here (otherwise, the experts will remind us!). The histograms imply that the long and short putts that end up into the hole are mostly from two feet, while most of the missed putts are 20 feet way. Next, it is hard to predict missed putts beyond 20 feet, as shown by the probability plot for missed putts. Finally, the boxplots (last chart) yields the same conclusion as the histograms.

 

 

 

 

 Conclusion

This quick review seems to tell us, in part, that it is good practice to place ball at about 2two feet from the whole. In contrast, a putt from 20 feet or more will more likely have to be putted again. Further investigation of how putts that went beyond the hole fared would be an interesting topic for further investigation.
